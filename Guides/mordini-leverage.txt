Leverage, in this context, is about Mark's downside avoidance — not Terry's upside recovery. Mark will pay a premium to make specific risks go away. The whole game is figuring out which risks are genuinely existential to him, which are merely expensive, and credibly signaling your access to them without crossing the line into something that looks like extortion.
Here's the ranked inventory, most leverage first.
Tier 1 — Existential to Mark's Career
ARDC exposure is your single biggest lever, by a wide margin.
Mark is a licensed Illinois attorney. His bar license is his livelihood, his identity, and the collateral behind every future dollar he earns. An ARDC investigation doesn't have to result in discipline to hurt him — the mere fact of an open file creates mandatory disclosure obligations (to malpractice carrier, to prospective insurance renewals, to courts on some pro hac vice applications), is public-adjacent, and is professionally humiliating. Once filed, it cannot be withdrawn by the complainant; the ARDC decides whether to close it.
The conduct you have in writing touches multiple Rules of Professional Conduct:
•	Rule 8.4(c) — dishonesty, fraud, deceit, or misrepresentation. The Mar 13, 2025 "no money for you today. Password needs to be fixed first" text is an attorney using his position to withhold wages in exchange for labor. That's not a close 8.4(c) call.
•	Rule 5.3 — responsibilities regarding non-lawyer assistance. Mark had supervisory obligations over your work; the record shows he was failing at basic supervision and offloading substantive work (discovery drafting, pleadings, client communication) onto you without proper oversight.
•	Rule 1.3 / 1.4 — diligence and client communication. The Sedeno default on 3/12/25 is a direct client-harm event. That is a separate malpractice-and-discipline problem Mark has completely apart from your wage claim.
•	Rule 8.4(b) — criminal act that reflects adversely on honesty or fitness. Willful failure to pay wages can be criminally prosecuted under 820 ILCS 115/14 (Class B misdemeanor for first offense, Class A misdemeanor for repeated offenses, potential Class 4 felony if the amount exceeds $10,000). The "I've already spoken to an attorney / I'll go to IRS and ARDC" exchange happened because Mark was, in real time, committing the conduct.
Important ethical line for you: You cannot explicitly condition not-filing-an-ARDC-complaint on being paid. That looks like extortion and, if a lawyer were doing it, would itself be an ARDC violation under Rule 8.4. Counsel sending a demand letter that references "all available remedies including regulatory complaints" is fine; you personally texting Mark "pay me or I file ARDC" is not. This is the single biggest reason to let a lawyer front the communication.
Tier 1 — Existential to Mark's Finances
IRS misclassification and payroll-tax exposure is your second-biggest lever.
Mark has been treating you as 1099 while directing your daily work, using his firm systems, setting your hours, and integrating you into core operations. That's textbook misclassification. The IRS penalty structure stacks:
•	Unpaid employer-side FICA (7.65% of wages, plus interest)
•	Unpaid federal income tax withholding (employer becomes liable)
•	Trust Fund Recovery Penalty under 26 U.S.C. § 6672 — 100% of unpaid trust-fund taxes, imposed personally on the responsible individual (Mark). TFRP is not dischargeable in bankruptcy and cannot be offloaded onto the PC.
•	State equivalents under Illinois withholding and unemployment-insurance statutes.
•	Failure-to-file and failure-to-pay penalties (5% per month each, capped).
Plus the IRS Whistleblower Program (Form 211) pays you 15–30% of collected proceeds on claims over $2 million in aggregate tax/penalties — that threshold is unlikely here but the program exists at lower percentages for smaller claims (Section 7623(a) discretionary awards). The real leverage isn't whether you'd ever actually collect; it's that Mark doesn't want a Form SS-8 determination or a Form 211 in his file.
Similar exposure at the state level: Illinois Department of Employment Security (unpaid UI premiums), Illinois Department of Revenue (withholding), Illinois Department of Labor (misclassification penalties under the Employee Classification Act in construction contexts; general misclassification penalties under IWPCA).
Tier 2 — Major Financial/Operational Threats
The fee-shift asymmetry. Every statute you could plead — IWPCA § 14, FLSA § 216(b), FWPA, Illinois Whistleblower Act, Personnel Record Review Act — has a prevailing-party attorney's-fee provision running one way (in your favor, not Mark's). This is massive. It means if you file and win even modestly, Mark pays your lawyer too, and the fee bill likely exceeds the wage principal. It also means competent employment counsel will take your case on contingency. Mark knows this. You should make sure Mark knows that you know this.
Malpractice carrier notification. Most ARDC complaints, IRS audits, and significant employment lawsuits trigger mandatory disclosure on Mark's next malpractice renewal application. Carriers frequently non-renew or hike premiums substantially after disclosure events. For a solo practitioner, loss of malpractice coverage is effectively loss of ability to practice.
First Chicago / CIC retainer relationship. This is the firm's revenue lifeline. If First Chicago becomes aware of (a) missed deadlines like the Sedeno default, (b) staff turnover and wage complaints, (c) ARDC investigation, or (d) operational chaos documented in your texts — they can pull the business at will. You're not going to tell First Chicago anything, but the possibility that discovery in your lawsuit would surface communications with CIC, or that an ARDC proceeding could prompt CIC inquiries, is a risk Mark has to price.
Tier 3 — Structural Pressure
Time asymmetry. IWPCA's 5% monthly penalty is accruing right now against Mark. Every month you wait, his exposure grows. By contrast, your SOL runway is 5–10 years on most theories — you have all the time in the world. Mark has no cash flow. He can't fund his own defense, let alone yours.
The documentary record. You have 65 screenshots of Mark admitting payroll failures, calling them a "broken record," and trying to condition wages on work performance. Mark has no explanation for these that survives cross-examination. The existence of this record means his settlement calculus cannot rely on "she said / he said" discounting.
The pattern of off-the-books payments. Cash in a drawer, Zelle from personal accounts, check mixing — this evidences payroll-tax non-compliance independently of your wage claim. Mark has to assume that anything he discloses in defending himself could expose him further on the IRS front.
Tier 4 — Soft / Situational
Your operational knowledge. You know where the bodies are buried — case status, filing deadlines, password conventions, SharePoint structure, QuickBooks. This is leverage in the sense that an orderly handover is something Mark needs from you. Do not use this as hostage-style leverage ("pay me or I won't hand over credentials"); that's a one-way ticket to a conversion or computer-fraud countersuit. It's leverage in the sense that a clean transition provision in the severance is something you can offer Mark in exchange for money, not withhold for money.
Continuity leverage. If Mark still wants you working on anything — signature runs, transitional filing help, training a replacement — he has to pay for that separately, and that's ongoing leverage.

The Single Highest-Leverage Play
Have employment counsel send a comprehensive demand letter that accomplishes five things in one document:
1.	Itemizes the IWPCA, FWPA, retaliation, oral-contract, quantum meruit, and §9.5 expense-reimbursement claims with dollar figures.
2.	References, without threatening, the "regulatory and disciplinary remedies available to the complainant" (ARDC and IRS are understood without being named as threats).
3.	Attaches a damages calculation showing realistic exposure at trial, including fee-shift.
4.	Makes a global settlement offer with a 14–21 day deadline and a proposed mutual release.
5.	Invokes statutory document-preservation obligations (which Mark is already violating by having no payroll records).
That letter does 80% of the work. Mark's options narrow to (a) retain counsel himself (which he can't afford and which starts the clock on his attorney explaining to him that he should settle), (b) ignore it and have you file, or (c) negotiate.
Option (c) is what you want.
The Ask Range
You haven't given me the dollar shortfall figure, so I'm working off the litigation-exposure framework I set out in § 6.4 of the revised analysis. Let me plug in illustrative numbers assuming ~$15K in unpaid straight-time wages (adjust up or down based on your reconstruction):
•	Unpaid wages: $15,000
•	IWPCA 5% monthly penalty × 15 months: ~$11,250
•	FWPA double damages in alternative framing: ~$15,000
•	FLSA liquidated damages if overtime holds: $5,000–$10,000
•	Expense reimbursement (§9.5): ~$500
•	Retaliation damages (lost wages + distress): $5,000–$15,000
•	Attorney fee-shift: $15,000–$25,000
Realistic trial exposure for Mark: $65K–$95K.
Typical settlement discount for clean resolution before filing: 30–50% off realistic trial exposure. That puts a reasonable severance ask in the $35K–$55K range for a global release, mutual non-disparagement, and neutral reference. If you want to anchor high, open at $65–75K. If you want to close fast, open at $45K and be willing to take $30–35K.
Non-dollar terms to negotiate for separately:
•	Mutual general release (you get the wage-claim release; Mark gets the ARDC/IRS/malpractice-witness non-pursuit, but see caveat below)
•	Mutual non-disparagement with carve-outs for truthful testimony under compulsion
•	Neutral reference clause specifying agreed-upon language
•	Confidentiality of settlement terms (Mark will want this; it's valuable to him)
•	1099 treatment of non-wage portion (liquidated damages and attorney-fee reimbursement are arguably non-wage; unpaid wages should be W-2'd with proper withholding — this matters for your tax posture)
•	Return of personal property / personal files
•	Agreed scope of work transition if any
Critical Traps to Avoid
Cannot release claims you're legally obligated to report. You can settle and release your wage claims. You cannot contractually agree to never cooperate with an IRS audit, ARDC investigation, or subpoena. If Mark's draft release tries to extract that, it's void as against public policy under the Defend Trade Secrets Act whistleblower provisions, Illinois Workplace Transparency Act (820 ILCS 96/), and federal SEC whistleblower rules. A well-drafted release will have carve-outs for this; a badly-drafted one won't, and you should reject that language.
Don't self-trigger extortion exposure. Do not text Mark anything that looks like "pay me $X or I file ARDC/IRS." The March 13 language you already sent (in the heat of the moment, responding to active wage theft) is defensible. A cold, deliberate pay-or-else text is not. Let counsel do the formal communications.
Illinois Workplace Transparency Act limits what Mark can get from you. Illinois severely limits non-disparagement and confidentiality clauses in employment settlements involving harassment or discrimination claims. Your claims are wage-and-retaliation, not harassment, so the WTA is less restrictive here — but if any part of this touches discriminatory treatment, Mark has to meet specific disclosure requirements for the NDA to be enforceable.
Preserve evidence before signaling. Export the full iMessage thread to a forensically-clean PDF with native metadata before sending any demand. If Mark decides to get nasty and wipe communications, you want the record already preserved and in counsel's hands.
Do not negotiate directly if you can avoid it. Every text you send Mark during negotiation becomes an exhibit. Every text Mark sends you becomes leverage. But you are better off having counsel send and receive, because (a) counsel can say things you can't (like referencing ARDC without it looking like a threat), and (b) you avoid inadvertently saying something that caps your own damages or looks like a compromise admission.
The Closing Observation
The single most valuable sentence in your whole case is this one: "I have 1900 for you." Mark wrote it on February 6, 2025. It's not a rhetorical flourish. It's a signed IOU. Every negotiation conversation with Mark's side should be anchored against the fact that Mark himself, in writing, acknowledged specific amounts owed and then didn't pay them. You don't have a messy factual dispute. You have a collection problem against a debtor who has already confessed.
That's the leverage. Now price it.
